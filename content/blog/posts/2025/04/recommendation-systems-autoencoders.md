## Motivation

Collaborative filtering methods such as matrix factorization have long been the backbone of recommendation systems. However, they struggle with sparse interaction data and cold-start scenarios. Deep learning — in particular, autoencoder architectures — offers a powerful alternative by learning dense latent representations of users and items directly from raw interaction signals.

## Autoencoder Architecture

The core idea is to train an encoder-decoder network to reconstruct a user's interaction vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{I}|}$ (ratings, clicks, purchases) through a low-dimensional bottleneck:

$$
\hat{\mathbf{x}} = f_\theta(g_\phi(\mathbf{x}))
$$

where $g_\phi$ is the encoder mapping to the latent space $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and $f_\theta$ is the decoder reconstructing the full interaction vector. Recommendations are generated by ranking the reconstructed scores $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ for unseen items.

## Training at Scale

In production, interaction matrices are extremely sparse — a user may have interacted with fewer than 0.1% of the item catalog. The loss function must account for this imbalance. A masked reconstruction loss over observed interactions works well:

$$
\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{O}_u|} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{O}_u} (x_{ui} - \hat{x}_{ui})^2
$$

where $\mathcal{O}_u$ is the set of observed interactions for user $u$.

## Deployment

The trained encoder is used offline to generate user embeddings, stored in a vector database. At serving time, approximate nearest-neighbor search retrieves candidate items, which are then re-ranked by the decoder — keeping inference latency within single-digit milliseconds.
